
Keep a separate document (could be a `.txt`, a notebook page, anything) where you write down, in real time as you read, any line or claim you couldn't follow or verify.

For example, while reading the proof of the First Main Theorem, you might encounter something like:

> _"Line 863: Why does $\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\rho \in \mathrm{PSH}(X,\phi)$ follow immediately? Is it just because $v_\beta \in \mathrm{PSH}(X,\phi)$ and $\rho = \frac{\alpha}{\beta}v_\beta$?"_

You write that down and keep reading. You don't stop to resolve it immediately.

The idea is that 
* If you stop to resolve every doubt as it appears, you lose the thread of the whole argument. 
* But if you ignore doubts entirely, they accumulate silently and your understanding has hidden gaps.

So the questions file serves two purposes:

1. **Short term** — lets you keep moving without losing your place
2. **Long term** — as you write more notes and understand more, you go back to the file and find that many questions answer themselves. The ones that don't are genuinely interesting — either a subtle point in the paper, a gap in your background, or an actual error.

In practice it could be as simple as a text file with entries like:

```
Line 651 — why is it λ_Y and not λ in eq.(G-new0)?
Line 1016 — the estimate in Theorem jet is just referenced, 
            where exactly in Bern20 does this come from?
Line 927 — the PSH claim for v_α, need to read Wang23 §2
```

Nothing elaborate. Just a live record of friction points as you read.
